Overview

Ten years of craft.
Every detail intentional.

The Pastry Kitchen began as a professional pastry operation and grew into a fully realized creative brand — one built on the same principles that define good UX: deep user research, iterative refinement, and a relentless commitment to the finished experience.

Every element of the brand — from the crimson palette to the typography to the web presence at thepastrykitchen.net — was designed from scratch to communicate warmth, precision, and artisan quality. This is the story of building that identity, and how it shaped the designer I am today.

Legacy & Process

The process behind
every piece.

Long before "design thinking" entered my vocabulary, the Pastry Kitchen ran on the same principles. Here is how the work actually happened.

Phase 01 · Discovery

Deep client research before a single sketch

Every engagement began with a thorough client consultation — understanding the vision, the venue, the palette, the guest count, the formality, and the story the couple wanted to tell. This is user research. The deliverable is just edible.

Phase 02 · Ideation

Iterative sketches reviewed with the client

Multiple design directions were sketched and presented. Client feedback shaped each revision. Nothing went to production without buy-in. The parallels to wireframing and design critique are exact — just with fondant instead of Figma.

Phase 03 · Production

High-precision execution under deadline pressure

Wedding timelines are unforgiving. Every element — structure, flavor, decoration, transport — had to be planned and executed with zero tolerance for failure. Stakeholder expectations were sky-high. Delivery had to be flawless.

Phase 04 · Delivery & Reflection

The moment of truth — and what came after

Every delivery became a retrospective. What worked? What would be designed differently? That culture of honest evaluation — of measuring finished work against original intent — is the root of how I approach UX today.

Phase 05 · Recognition

A.S. Baking & Pastry Arts — Honors, Silver Medalist

Sullivan University, Louisville, KY. The Silver Medal is a signal — that the craft was pursued to a standard beyond the minimum. That standard is still the baseline.

Key Decisions

What I chose — and why.

Every brand and design decision was intentional. These are the choices that shaped the identity and what they communicate about the thinking behind the work.

01
Color

Crimson over pastel

Many pastry brands default to pinks and creams. The choice to anchor the palette in deep crimson was a deliberate signal: this is a professional operation, not a hobby. Warmth without softness.

02
Platform

Custom build over template

Rather than using a generic Wix or Squarespace template, the site was built and deployed on Netlify via GitHub — giving full control over performance, structure, and brand expression. The technical investment paid off in brand integrity.

03
Architecture

PK Designs as parent brand

Rather than treating the Pastry Kitchen as a standalone, it was positioned as the first leg of PK Designs — a scalable creative brand system with room for a watercolor print shop and 3D print brand. Design systems thinking from day one.

04
Narrative

Process as the differentiator

Many cake businesses show the finished product. The Pastry Kitchen documents the process — sketches, consultations, iterations. This builds trust and positions the work as design-led, not just craft-led.

05
Typography

Serif as tone-setter

Playfair Display was chosen for its editorial weight — the kind of typeface you find in premium publications. It communicates experience and taste without pretension. A deliberate departure from the script fonts common in the space.

06
Portfolio

Integration into the design narrative

The Pastry Kitchen's work is embedded directly into the davidrbailey.com portfolio — not as a curiosity, but as evidence that UX principles predate the tools. The "Three Careers, One Through Line" narrative makes it the strongest argument in the deck.

The Bridge

Pastry design is
UX design.

This isn't a metaphor. It's a literal description of the same process — client research, iterative design, stakeholder management, and high-stakes delivery — applied to a different medium.

Pastry Kitchen

Client consultation · Sketch iteration · Structural planning · Flavor system design · Deadline-driven delivery · Post-event reflection

⇄

UX/UI Design

User research · Wireframe iteration · Information architecture · Design system creation · Sprint-driven delivery · Retrospective analysis

  • Client empathy → User empathy Reading a bride's vision from a three-word brief taught me that the stated request is never the whole picture. UX interviews work the same way.
  • Sketch → Wireframe Multiple design directions on paper before committing to any one. The medium changed; the discipline didn't.
  • Structural engineering → Information architecture A five-tier cake has to hold. So does a five-level navigation hierarchy. Both require thinking about load, flow, and failure modes.
  • Decoration decisions → Visual hierarchy Where does the eye land first? What communicates premium quality immediately? These are design questions — whether the canvas is fondant or a screen.
  • Wedding deadline → Sprint deadline No extensions. No "let's push it a week." The discipline of planning every phase backward from a hard delivery date is directly transferable to product timelines.
  • Client feedback loops → Design critique Presenting multiple options, receiving feedback, iterating, and presenting again. The cadence of a design review and a cake consultation are identical in structure.
